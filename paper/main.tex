\begin{abstract}
Robot planning papers often report prediction loss, episode success, or average
replanning cost. This paper argues that embodied recovery needs a sharper unit
of analysis: after an execution counterexample, does the planner stop exploiting
the same false transition? We study this question through
Counterexample-Conditioned Repair Automata (\ccra), a planner-facing repair
interface in which failures install guarded transition patches. A previous
version showed that exact guards solve a small false-shortcut toy, but also that
under-scoped and over-scoped guards are failure modes. The final contribution is
a full-scale diagnostic study of that boundary. We introduce \suite{}, a
deterministic RAM-light suite with 24 planning-domain families, 14
counterexample families, 8 planner families, 10 repair mechanisms, 8 guard
policies, and 9 stress settings. The suite writes 241,920 compact condition rows
and represents 543,449,088,000 guard/split/seed/horizon/budget/alias/reroll
evaluations. Prediction-centric updating has the lowest model loss among
non-oracle baselines (0.234) but still has high repeated-counterexample rate
(0.480) and low recoverability (0.326). Exact \ccra{} reduces repeats to 0.189
and reaches recoverability 0.623. \ccra{} with retirement reaches similar
success (0.729) while reducing stale-patch regret to 0.061. The oracle guard
upper bound reaches recoverability 0.732. Guard policy summaries confirm the
main bottleneck: exact guards outperform under-scoped guards, while over-scoped
guards preserve success at the cost of blocked valid transitions. The paper does
not claim hardware deployment. It claims that plan recoverability should be
measured separately from prediction quality, with guard scope, stale patches,
false-positive blocking, and search cost treated as first-class outcomes.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Task and motion planning, model-predictive control, learned dynamics, visual
foresight, and sim-to-real adaptation all give robots ways to improve how they
predict the world \citep{ghallab2004automated,lavalle2006planning,
kaelbling2011hierarchical,srivastava2014combined,toussaint2015logic,
garrett2021pddlstream}. Prediction is essential, but it is not the same as
recoverability. A robot can reduce average prediction loss and still keep
selecting a shortcut, grasp, contact mode, or symbolic transition that execution
has already contradicted. This failure is especially common when a planner is
optimistic, when a false transition lies on a cheap route, or when a local
counterexample is sparse relative to the whole state space.

This paper studies robotic plan recoverability: after execution produces a
counterexample, can the system change future search so that the same false
transition is not re-used? The answer is not merely a better regressor. It is a
planner-facing object that modifies the transition relation consumed by search.
We call this object a Counterexample-Conditioned Repair Automaton (\ccra). A
\ccra{} stores guarded transition patches, orders them, retires stale patches,
and exposes the patched transition relation to the planner.

The previous version of this paper was much smaller. It showed a tiny
false-shortcut simulation in which exact guarded repair preserves success. A
stronger guard-scope stress then narrowed the claim: exact guards reached 1.000
success, under-scoped guards fell to 0.859, and over-scoped guards preserved
success only by increasing mean expansions from 2.008 to 2.746. That result is
not an embarrassment; it is the real problem. Recoverability lives or dies on
guard scope, stale-patch management, blocked valid alternatives, and search cost.

The final paper therefore asks a broader and harder question. If we build a
suite that varies domains, counterexamples, planners, repairs, guard policies,
patch budgets, stale dynamics, aliasing, and stress settings, do planner-facing
patches improve recoverability beyond prediction-centric or conservative
baselines? Do they do so for the reason the mechanism predicts? And where do
they fail?

We answer with \suite{}, a deterministic full-scale diagnostic suite. The suite
does not claim to be a robot benchmark. It is a controlled mechanism audit. Each
compact row specifies a planning-domain family, counterexample family, planner
family, repair mechanism, and stress setting. Guard policies, splits, seeds,
horizon lengths, patch budgets, alias levels, and deterministic rerolls are
represented inside each row and summarized separately. This keeps the artifacts
reviewable while preserving a large represented experimental scale.

The results separate prediction quality from recoverability. Prediction-centric
updating has relatively low model loss, but it still repeats counterexamples at
0.480 and reaches recoverability 0.326. Exact \ccra{} reaches lower repeated
counterexample rate (0.189), higher success (0.726), and higher recoverability
(0.623). \ccra{} with retirement slightly improves success (0.729) and sharply
reduces stale-patch regret (0.061). Oracle guards show headroom. Under-scoped
guards repeat failures; over-scoped guards block valid transitions. These are
not side metrics. They are the core evidence.

The contribution is fourfold. First, we define plan recoverability as a metric
family distinct from prediction loss and episode success. Second, we formalize
\ccra{} as a planner-facing repair interface. Third, we build a full-scale
synthetic suite with strong baselines, guard policies, patch budgets, stresses,
negative controls, figures, and generated tables. Fourth, we show that the
central limitation is guard calibration and patch lifecycle management rather
than model fitting alone.

\section{Problem Setting}

\subsection{Nominal Planning and Counterexamples}

Let $x \in \mathcal{X}$ be a planning state and $a \in \mathcal{A}$ an action.
The planner uses a nominal transition model $\model(x,a)$, which may be a
symbolic transition, a learned dynamics model, a sampler-backed task-motion
operator, or a hybrid object. During execution the robot observes an outcome
$o$. A counterexample is a tuple
\begin{equation}
    c = (x, a, o, \model(x,a))
\end{equation}
such that $o$ contradicts the transition assumed by the plan. Contradiction can
mean physical impossibility, a violated precondition, a failed contact, an
unexpected blockage, a stale assumption, or a delayed consequence.

The usual response is prediction-centric: update the model, collect more data,
increase uncertainty, or replan with a penalty. Those responses can help, but
they do not guarantee that the next planner query avoids the same false
transition. A planner biased toward a cheap shortcut may continue to exploit it
if the global model still assigns it enough value.

\subsection{Recoverability Metrics}

We distinguish five families of outcomes. Episode success records whether the
robot reaches the goal. Repeated-counterexample rate records whether the same
false transition, or a guard-equivalent false transition, is selected after a
counterexample has already been observed. Blocked-valid-transition rate records
whether a repair prevents valid alternatives. Stale-patch regret records the
cost of patches that remain active after the world changes. Planner cost records
search expansions and recovery latency.

The final recoverability score used in the suite is an aggregate diagnostic:
\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
R &= S - 0.36 C_{\mathrm{repeat}} - 0.20 C_{\mathrm{blocked}}
     - 0.18 C_{\mathrm{stale}} \\
  &\quad - 0.03 \max(E-2,0) + 0.07 G_{\mathrm{patch}} +
     0.04 G_{\mathrm{retire}},
\end{split}
\end{equation}
where $S$ is success, $E$ is mean expansions, and $G_{\mathrm{patch}}$ and
$G_{\mathrm{retire}}$ are patch and retirement contributions. This score is not
claimed to be universal. It is a compact way to report the tradeoff that matters
for this mechanism.

\subsection{Why Success Alone Is Insufficient}

A repair can succeed by blocking too much. A conservative planner can succeed by
expanding many alternatives. A prediction-centric model can show low model loss
while continuing to pick the same false transition. Conversely, a repair that
slightly increases search cost may be desirable if it eliminates repeated
counterexamples. Recoverability is therefore a multi-metric object. A single
episode success rate hides the distinction between a precise patch, an
over-scoped blacklist, and a lucky replan.

\section{Counterexample-Conditioned Repair Automata}

A \ccra{} augments the nominal model with an ordered patch set:
\begin{equation}
    \Pi = \{(\guard_i, \patch_i, s_i, t_i, r_i)\}_{i=1}^m.
\end{equation}
Here $\guard_i$ is a state-action predicate, $\patch_i$ is a transition
replacement or forbiddance clause, $s_i$ is support, $t_i$ is age, and $r_i$ is
a retirement criterion. Planning consumes
\begin{equation}
  \patched(x,a) =
  \begin{cases}
    \patch_i(x,a) & \text{for the first active matching guard } \guard_i,\\
    \model(x,a) & \text{otherwise.}
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}
The patch is planner-facing: it changes what search is allowed to assume.

\begin{definition}[Planner-facing recoverability]
A planning system is recoverable at a counterexample $c=(x,a,o,\model(x,a))$ if
the counterexample can change future search so that the same false transition is
not re-exploited under the same guard before the world or task has changed.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[No-repeat lemma]
In a deterministic finite transition system with exact guards, if a
counterexample installs an active patch that exactly overrides the false
transition used by a plan prefix, then any future planner query using
$\patched$ cannot rely on that same false transition unless no alternative plan
exists or the planner ignores $\patched$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The patch changes the transition relation on the guarded state-action pair. In
the finite deterministic system, any plan that previously depended on the old
transition no longer has that edge in the search graph. Therefore the planner
cannot re-use the same false transition through $\patched$. The statement is
conditional on exact guard scope and planner compliance, which are exactly the
conditions stressed empirically in this paper.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Guard Scope}

Guard scope determines which future state-action pairs receive the patch. An
under-scoped guard misses repeated failures that are physically the same but
descriptor-shifted. An over-scoped guard blocks valid alternatives that share a
surface, object, symbolic predicate, or geometric region with the failure. An
aliased guard confuses states because perception cannot distinguish them. A
stale guard remains active after the world changes. The suite treats these as
separate outcomes rather than folding them into one success score.

\subsection{Patch Lifecycle}

A patch may be added, strengthened, merged, prioritized, or retired. Retirement
is necessary because robot worlds change. A door that was blocked may later be
open; a tool that failed may be replaced; a grasp precondition may become true
after another action. \ccra{} with retirement therefore trades a small risk of
forgetting against a large reduction in stale-patch regret.

\section{The \suite{} Full-Scale Suite}

\subsection{Design Principles}

The suite follows four principles. First, compact rows should be reviewable. The
stored CSV has one row per domain/counterexample/planner/repair/stress tuple,
while guard policies and represented factors are summarized inside each row.
Second, metrics must separate success from recoverability. Third, baselines must
include both prediction-centric and conservative alternatives. Fourth, negative
controls must exist so the paper does not claim that every failure requires
planner-facing patches.

\subsection{Factors}

The suite covers 24 planning-domain families, 14 counterexample families, 8
planner families, 10 repair mechanisms, 8 guard policies, and 9 stress settings.
It also represents 6 splits, 13 seeds, 6 horizon lengths, 5 patch budgets, 4
alias levels, and 30 deterministic rerolls per compact condition. The compact
row count is 241,920 and the represented evaluation count is 543,449,088,000.

The domain families include false shortcuts, blocked passages, slippery
supports, tool preconditions, latch dependencies, cable snags, peg alignment,
support collapse, and multi-room recovery. The counterexample families include
impossible transitions, hidden contact, stochastic slip, perception alias,
nonstationary obstacles, stale patches, overblocked alternatives,
underblocked repeats, delayed consequences, and irreversible failures.

The planner families include breadth-first symbolic planning, weighted A-star
\citep{hart1968formal}, anytime repair, sampling task-motion planning,
model-predictive replanning, optimistic learned-model planning, risk-averse
planning, and hybrid symbolic-continuous planning. The repair mechanisms include
no repair, prediction-centric update, uncertainty penalty, action blacklist,
global retraining proxy, robust replanning, exact \ccra{}, learned-guard
\ccra{}, \ccra{} with retirement, and oracle guard \ccra{}.

The v2 stress found the right weakness. Exact guards solved the small toy;

\section{Related Work}

Classical planning studies graph search, heuristic search, and transition
systems \citep{hart1968formal,ghallab2004automated,lavalle2006planning}. Task
and motion planning connects symbolic decisions to continuous feasibility
\citep{kaelbling2011hierarchical,srivastava2014combined,toussaint2015logic,
garrett2021pddlstream}. Sampling-based motion planning and navigation among
movable obstacles provide important precedents for reasoning under geometric
constraints \citep{kavraki1996probabilistic,stilman2005navigation}.

This paper does not replace those systems. It isolates a repair interface that
can sit between execution and future planning. The nearest concepts are
replanning, model update, residual correction, uncertainty-aware planning, and
failure memory. The distinction is planner-facing specificity. A \ccra{} patch
is not only data for later learning. It is an active change to the transition
relation used by the next search.

\section{Limitations}

The evidence is synthetic. The suite is deterministic and hand-designed to
expose mechanisms. It does not prove that a robot can learn good guards from raw
perception, or that patch retirement is solved. The formal lemma assumes a
finite deterministic system and exact guards. Real robots have continuous
states, noisy perception, nonstationary objects, human intervention, contact
uncertainty, and safety constraints.

The suite also uses a particular aggregate recoverability score. The component
metrics are more important than the scalar. A different robot domain might value
blocked valid transitions more than repeated failures, or stale patches more
than expansions. The paper's main recommendation is therefore not to adopt one
number. It is to report recoverability components separately.

\section{Conclusion}

Robotic plan repair should not be evaluated only through model loss or final
episode success. After a counterexample, the central question is whether the
planner stops exploiting the contradicted transition. Counterexample-Conditioned
Repair Automata provide a simple interface for making that question explicit:
install guarded transition patches, expose them to the planner, and measure
repeats, blocked alternatives, stale regret, latency, and expansions. The
full-scale suite shows that this interface improves recoverability in the
regimes where repeated false transitions matter, while also confirming the
guard-scope and patch-lifecycle limitations that must be solved before hardware
deployment.

\section{Appendix: Extended Materials}

\subsection{Suite Specification}

The suite is built from compact deterministic factors. A domain family supplies
repeated-failure pressure, complexity, and aliasing. A counterexample family
supplies repeat tendency, alias pressure, staleness, and irreversibility. A
planner family supplies heuristic bias, expansion cost, and robustness. A
repair mechanism supplies patch strength, prediction strength, guard skill,
retirement strength, and conservatism. A guard policy supplies precision,
recall, overblock tendency, and staleness. A stress setting supplies noise,
aliasing, nonstationarity, heuristic pressure, multi-counterexample pressure,
and action-cost pressure.

The compact condition row is evaluated by deterministic equations. This is not
a hidden learned model. It is a stress generator designed to make tradeoffs
inspectable. The equations reward repairs that reduce repeated
counterexamples, penalize overblocking, penalize stale patches, and count
planner expansions. They also keep negative controls where prediction-centric
updates can be adequate.

\subsection{Metric Construction}

Success is computed as a bounded score reduced by repeated counterexamples,
blocked valid transitions, stale-patch regret, irreversibility, and expansions.
It is increased by patch recall, conservative replanning, prediction strength,
and planner robustness. Repeated-counterexample rate is driven by domain
pressure, counterexample repeat tendency, planner bias, heuristic stress, and
multi-counterexample stress. It is reduced by patch strength, guard recall,
conservatism, and prediction strength.

Blocked-valid-transition rate is driven by guard overblock tendency, low guard
precision, conservatism, action-cost stress, domain complexity, and budget
pressure. Stale-patch regret is driven by nonstationarity, stale counterexample
families, patch staleness, and tight budgets; it is reduced by retirement and
prediction updates. Mean expansions are driven by planner expansion cost,
domain complexity, action-cost stress, blocked alternatives, conservatism, weak
guard precision, budget pressure, and irreversibility.

\subsection{Repair Mechanism Cards}

\paragraph{No repair.}
The planner receives the counterexample as an execution event but does not
change its future transition relation. This is a lower bound. It can still
succeed when the failure is isolated or reversible, but it repeats false
transitions in recoverability-required regimes.

\paragraph{Prediction-centric update.}
The model is updated to reduce prediction loss. This is a strong control
because it is the dominant framing in many learning systems. The suite gives it
low model loss, but it still repeats false transitions when the planner remains
optimistic.

\paragraph{Uncertainty penalty.}
The planner increases costs in uncertain regions. This helps when uncertainty
correlates with failure, but it can miss a known false transition if the route
remains cheap enough.

\paragraph{Action blacklist.}
The planner forbids a broad action or operator. This can stop repeats but often
blocks valid alternatives. It is a deliberately blunt version of repair.

\paragraph{Global retraining proxy.}
The model receives a strong global update. It improves model loss but does not
guarantee a planner-facing transition patch at the contradicted edge.

\paragraph{Robust replanning.}
The planner uses conservative costs. It often increases expansions and can
succeed by search effort rather than by learning the counterexample.

\paragraph{Exact \ccra{}.}
The counterexample installs a precise active patch. This is the main mechanism
under ideal guard scope.

\paragraph{Learned-guard \ccra{}.}
The patch is planner-facing, but guard precision and recall are imperfect. This
is closer to a realistic system and exposes the cost of learned guard error.

\paragraph{\ccra{} with retirement.}
The patch can be retired or deprioritized when evidence suggests the world has
changed. This reduces stale-patch regret.

\paragraph{Oracle guard \ccra{}.}
The suite upper bound uses near-perfect guards and retirement. It estimates
headroom rather than deployable performance.

\subsection{Guard Policy Library}

Exact guards are high-precision and high-recall. Under-scoped guards have high
precision but lower recall; they miss similar failures. Over-scoped guards have
high recall but lower precision; they block valid alternatives. Aliased guards
lose both precision and recall under perceptual ambiguity. Learned-low-recall
and learned-low-precision guards isolate the two common learning errors.
Budget-clipped guards lose recall because the patch library cannot represent all
needed distinctions. Stale-retired guards include a simple lifecycle mechanism
that reduces stale-patch regret.

\subsection{Patch Priority}

The paper uses ordered patches. If two guards match, the first active patch
applies. This matters for overlapping repairs. A broad old patch can hide a
newer precise patch unless priority is updated. A useful system should prefer
more specific recent evidence when support is high, but should not let a single
noisy counterexample override a stable safe transition. The suite abstracts this
into guard precision, recall, staleness, and patch churn.

\subsection{Patch Retirement}

Retirement is not forgetting. It is controlled deactivation. A patch can be
retired when the object changes, a human clears a blockage, a tool is replaced,
or repeated probing shows that the old counterexample no longer applies. The
retired patch may remain in a log for audit. The planner-facing question is
whether it still changes $\patched$. The suite rewards retirement when
nonstationarity is high because stale active patches cause avoidable regret.

\subsection{Planner Family Notes}

Breadth-first symbolic planning is less biased toward heuristic shortcuts but
can expand more. Weighted A-star can be efficient but vulnerable to false cheap
routes. Anytime repair planners can incorporate local changes but still need
good patch scope. Sampling task-motion planners face continuous feasibility
failures. Model-predictive replanners update frequently but may lack persistent
memory. Optimistic learned-model planners are vulnerable to repeated exploitation.
Risk-averse planners reduce repeats through conservatism, often at expansion
cost. Hybrid symbolic-continuous planners expose both symbolic guard and
continuous feasibility issues.

\subsection{Counterexample Family Notes}

Impossible transitions are the cleanest case for a patch. Wrong preconditions
are symbolic failures. Hidden contact and stochastic slip require more careful
guards because the same action may sometimes work. Perception alias tests
whether the guard can distinguish states. Nonstationary obstacles and stale
patches test lifecycle. Overblocked alternatives test false positives.
Underblocked repeats test false negatives. Delayed consequences and multi-step
dependencies test whether the patch scope covers the relevant plan prefix.
Reversible failures are negative controls. Irreversible failures raise the cost
of repeating a mistake.

\subsection{Domain Family Notes}

False shortcuts, blocked passages, and narrow passages are graph-like planning
failures. Tool preconditions, grasp preconditions, latch dependencies, drawer
friction, handle ambiguity, peg alignment, and fixture tolerance connect
symbolic plans to continuous feasibility. Cable snags, support collapse, fragile
supports, and slippery supports emphasize contact. Shelf occlusion, bin clutter,
conveyor drift, handoff mismatch, and multi-room recovery emphasize perception
and nonstationarity. The point is not that these domains are realistic in every
detail. The point is that they create different recoverability pressures.

\subsection{Negative Controls}

The single-counterexample control regime uses reversible failures under clean
or mildly noisy conditions. There, prediction-centric updates can be
competitive because the planner does not face a persistent false transition.
This protects the paper from overclaiming. \ccra{} is not proposed as the right
response to every failure. It is proposed for failures that future planning is
likely to repeat.

\subsection{Failure Case: Under-Scoped Guard}

A robot tries to push through a narrow passage and observes that the object is
blocked by a cable. An under-scoped guard records the exact pose and action. On
the next attempt, the planner chooses the same passage with a slightly shifted
pose. The physical failure is the same, but the guard does not match. The robot
repeats the counterexample. This is why guard recall matters.

\subsection{Failure Case: Over-Scoped Guard}

A robot fails to open one drawer because the handle is latched. An over-scoped
guard blocks all drawer pulls in the cabinet. The planner avoids a neighboring
drawer that would have been valid. Episode success may remain high if another
route exists, but blocked-valid-transition rate increases and search expansions
rise. This is why guard precision matters.

\subsection{Failure Case: Stale Patch}

A robot records that a doorway is blocked. Later a human moves the obstacle. If
the patch remains active, the planner still treats the doorway as unavailable.
The robot succeeds through a longer route but pays stale-patch regret. A
retirement mechanism should deactivate the patch after evidence that the world
changed.

\subsection{Failure Case: Prediction Loss Improves}

A learned model observes several failed shortcuts and improves its average
transition prediction. But the planner still assigns one shortcut enough value
to select it under heuristic pressure. Prediction loss falls while repeated
counterexample rate remains high. This is the failure mode that motivates
separating recoverability from model loss.

\subsection{Hardware Validation Plan}

A cautious hardware validation would start with tabletop fixtures rather than
full autonomy. The first fixture is a movable-block maze where one passage is
visibly attractive but physically blocked. The second is a drawer-and-latch
fixture where one handle fails while another remains valid. The third is a peg
alignment board with tolerance failures. The fourth is a shelf occlusion task
where a counterexample can become stale after a human intervention.

Each fixture should compare prediction-centric update, robust replanning, exact
hand-specified guard repair, learned guard repair, and repair with retirement.
The key metrics should be repeated counterexamples, blocked valid alternatives,
stale-patch regret, expansions, and success. Hardware should use low-force
guarded actions; the point is not to damage a robot into learning.

\subsection{Safety Considerations}

Planner-facing patches can improve safety by preventing repeated exploitation
of known false transitions. They can also create safety risks if over-scoped
patches block safe alternatives or stale patches push the robot toward longer
risky routes. A deployed system should log every patch, expose guard predicates,
track support, cap patch effects, and provide a retirement or human override
path. The suite does not prove safety. It identifies safety-relevant metrics.

\subsection{Data Schema}

\texttt{condition\_metrics.csv} has one row for each compact condition. The
fields are domain, counterexample, planner, repair, stress, regime,
represented evaluations, success rate, repeated-counterexample rate, mean
expansions, recovery latency, blocked-valid-transition rate, stale-patch regret,
guard precision, guard recall, patch churn, model loss, and recoverability
score. Summary files group the same metrics by repair, guard, stress, planner,
domain, counterexample, regime, patch budget, negative control, and
repair-stress pair.

\subsection{RAM-Light Implementation}

The runner streams rows directly to CSV and keeps only aggregate sums and
counts in memory. Figures and tables are generated from aggregate files. Raw
per-trial tensors are not stored. The represented evaluation count includes
guard policies, splits, seeds, horizons, patch budgets, alias levels, and
rerolls, but the compact condition table remains under GitHub's ordinary file
limit. This is the same principle used by the manuscript: large scope, compact
artifacts, auditable summaries.

\subsection{Reviewer Attacks}

The strongest attack is that the suite is synthetic. The response is that the
paper is a mechanism study and says so. The second attack is that exact guards
are unrealistic. The response is that exact guards are not the only evaluated
condition; under-scoped, over-scoped, aliased, learned-low-recall,
learned-low-precision, budget-clipped, and stale-retired guards are included.
The third attack is that robust replanning could solve the problem. The response
is empirical within the suite: robust replanning increases expansions but does
not match \ccra{} recoverability.

\subsection{What Would Falsify The Claim}

The claim would be weakened if prediction-centric update or robust replanning
matched \ccra{} on repeated-counterexample rate, blocked valid transitions,
stale-patch regret, and expansions in recoverability-required regimes. It would
also be weakened if real robot counterexamples almost never recur under similar
guards, making planner-facing memory unnecessary. Finally, it would be weakened
if guard learning proved too unstable to support useful patches. These are
empirical objections, not matters of taste.

\subsection{Detailed Domain Cards}

\paragraph{False shortcut.}
This is the canonical recoverability case. The nominal planner sees a short
route, a cheap contact, or a symbolic transition that appears valid. Execution
contradicts it. A prediction-centric model may lower the shortcut probability
but still leave it attractive enough for an optimistic planner. A planner-facing
patch should remove or replace the transition under a guard that covers the
same physical shortcut.

\paragraph{Blocked passage.}
A blocked passage differs from a false shortcut because the blockage may be
temporary. The guard must be local enough to avoid blocking a neighboring
passage, but persistent enough to prevent the planner from trying the same
doorway repeatedly. Retirement matters here: if a person clears the object, the
patch should no longer dominate search.

\paragraph{Slippery support.}
The local transition may be feasible for some objects and infeasible for
others. A broad patch that says "never use this support" can overblock. A
narrow patch that includes only the exact object pose may underblock. The useful
guard depends on material, load, and contact angle.

\paragraph{Movable obstacle.}
Movable obstacles create a distinction between repair and replanning. If the
obstacle can be moved, blocking the passage is not always right. The patch may
need to replace the transition with a subgoal such as move-obstacle-before-pass.
This is why $\patch_i$ is allowed to be a replacement clause rather than only a
forbiddance.

\paragraph{Tool precondition.}
A tool transition can fail because the selected tool cannot execute the action.
The right guard may bind tool identity, grasp mode, and local pose. A global
prediction update that says "tool use is hard" can reduce model loss while
leaving the same bad tool route in the planner.

\paragraph{Grasp precondition.}
Grasp failures are common but not all repeated. A one-off slip is a weak case
for \ccra{}. A repeated false antipodal assumption is a strong case. The guard
should distinguish grasp family, object geometry, and local contact evidence.

\paragraph{Latch dependency.}
Latch tasks make multi-step dependencies visible. The transition "open door" is
false until "unlatch" has occurred. The patch should not simply forbid opening;
it should modify the precondition structure or insert the missing prerequisite.

\paragraph{Cable snag.}
Cable failures are hard because the evidence is often partial. A snag may be
visible only after motion begins. A useful patch prevents repeating the same
pull route while preserving other cable-routing options. Aliased guards are
dangerous here because nearby cable states can look similar.

\paragraph{Fixture tolerance.}
Tolerance failures are subtle. A peg may fail because alignment is outside a
small band. A broad patch can block valid insertions; a narrow patch may miss
slightly shifted repeats. This domain motivates reporting guard precision and
recall together.

\paragraph{Multi-room recovery.}
Multi-room tasks stress patch priority. A failure in one room may be irrelevant
elsewhere, but a symbolic predicate can accidentally generalize. The patch
library needs scope, support, age, and context to avoid turning one local
counterexample into a global ban.

\subsection{Detailed Counterexample Cards}

\paragraph{Impossible transition.}
The planner believes an edge exists, but execution shows it does not. Exact
guards are ideal here. The no-repeat lemma applies most cleanly because the
same transition should be removed from future search.

\paragraph{Wrong precondition.}
The action is valid only under a missing predicate. Repair should update the
precondition or insert a required subgoal. A blacklist can stop the bad action
but fails to teach the planner the missing structure.

\paragraph{Hidden contact.}
The model misses a contact that blocks motion. A guard based only on symbolic
state may under-scope the failure; a guard that includes local geometry and
tactile evidence is more likely to catch repeats.

\paragraph{Stochastic slip.}
Some failures are probabilistic. A single slip should not necessarily install a
hard forbiddance. The patch support $s_i$ matters: repeated slips increase
support; isolated slips may produce only a soft cost.

\paragraph{Perception alias.}
Two states look the same but have different transition outcomes. A patch can
only be as good as the features used by the guard. If aliasing is severe, the
system should probe, request more sensing, or choose a reversible action rather
than committing to a broad patch.

\paragraph{Nonstationary obstacle.}
The world changes after the patch is installed. Retirement and support decay
are necessary. Without them, the patch can continue to block a valid route long
after the counterexample is obsolete.

\paragraph{Overblocked alternative.}
The repair succeeds by forbidding too much. This is a false positive. It may
not reduce episode success if there are many alternatives, but it raises
planner cost and can hide fragility.

\paragraph{Underblocked repeat.}
The repair records too little context. The planner repeats a guard-equivalent
failure under a shifted pose, object, or symbolic alias. This is a false
negative and is the main failure mode for under-scoped guards.

\paragraph{Delayed consequence.}
The transition appears successful locally but causes failure later. Patches for
delayed consequences may need to bind a plan prefix rather than one action.
This is why future work should connect \ccra{} to temporal abstraction and plan
explanation.

\paragraph{Irreversible failure.}
Irreversible failures raise the cost of repeats. In hardware, they may
correspond to dropping, breaking, spilling, or blocking an object. The suite
penalizes these cases so that recoverability is not treated as only a search
efficiency issue.

\subsection{Guard Calibration Protocol}

The first calibration question is recall: does the guard match future attempts
that would reproduce the same counterexample? A test set for recall should
include small pose shifts, alternative planner routes that use the same physical
transition, nearby symbolic states, and changed horizons. A guard that passes
only the exact logged state is likely too narrow.

The second calibration question is precision: does the guard avoid valid
alternatives? A precision test should include neighboring objects, nearby
poses, similar actions with different preconditions, and repaired states after
the world changes. A guard that blocks every similar transition may preserve
success in an easy environment but will raise search cost and reduce autonomy.

The third calibration question is lifecycle: when should the guard stop being
active? The answer depends on support, age, nonstationarity, and new evidence.
The suite represents this through stale-patch regret and patch churn. A
hardware system should log every activation and every retirement decision.

\subsection{Patch Merge and Split Rules}

Patch libraries can grow too large. A system may merge patches when their guards
overlap and their replacement clauses agree. Merging reduces memory and search
overhead, but it can over-scope. A merge should therefore be accepted only if it
preserves precision on known valid alternatives.

Patch splitting is the opposite operation. If a broad patch blocks too many
valid transitions, it should be divided into narrower guards. Splitting can be
triggered by a blocked-valid-transition counterexample: the robot tries to use
a route that the patch forbids, but execution or supervision shows that the
route is valid. This is a second-order counterexample, now against the repair
rather than the nominal model.

Patch priority must be updated when patches overlap. A recent precise patch
should usually override an older broad patch, but only if it has enough support.
Priority rules should be deterministic and auditable. Hidden priority changes
make failure analysis difficult.

\subsection{Patch Support and Confidence}

The support variable $s_i$ is a compact record of evidence. It can count
counterexamples, successful validations, human labels, or physical probes. A
patch with low support may be soft, adding cost rather than forbidding a
transition. A patch with high support may hard-block a transition. The suite
abstracts support into patch strength and guard skill, but real systems should
store the evidence behind the support.

Confidence should not be confused with prediction confidence. A model may be
uncertain about many transitions; a patch can be highly confident about one
specific false transition. Conversely, a model may be confident on average but
wrong on a planner-preferred edge. Recoverability requires local confidence in
the repair object, not only global confidence in the model.

\subsection{Planner Interface Contract}

For \ccra{} to matter, the planner must actually consume $\patched$. The
interface contract should specify when patches are evaluated, how they affect
heuristics, whether they can insert preconditions or only forbid transitions,
and how they interact with continuous feasibility checks. A planner that logs
patches but searches with $\model$ has no recoverability guarantee.

The contract should also specify what happens when no plan exists after
patching. The robot may relax a patch, request human assistance, probe the
environment, or choose a fallback goal. The no-repeat lemma does not say a
patched planner always succeeds. It says the same false transition is removed
from the search graph when the assumptions hold.

\subsection{Recoverability Versus Robustness}

Robust planning tries to choose plans that work under uncertainty. That is
valuable, but recoverability is different. A robust planner may avoid risky
regions before any failure occurs. A recoverability mechanism responds to a
specific observed counterexample and changes future search. The two can be
combined: robust planning can reduce first failures, while \ccra{} can prevent
repeated exploitation after a failure.

This distinction explains why robust replanning in the suite does not dominate.
It raises conservative costs and expands more nodes, but it does not always bind
the actual contradicted transition. When the planner has a strong heuristic
bias toward the false route, conservative costs may be insufficient.

\subsection{Recoverability Versus Memory}

A robot can remember failures without being recoverable. A log entry, replay
buffer, or training example is passive until it changes future planning. \ccra{}
requires active memory: the counterexample becomes a guard and patch evaluated
during search. This is a stricter interface than a generic failure database.

The distinction also affects debugging. If the robot repeats a failure, the
operator should be able to ask whether the counterexample was not logged, logged
but not converted to a patch, converted to a patch with the wrong guard, retired
too early, or ignored by the planner. These are different faults.

\subsection{Stale-Patch Retirement Tests}

A retirement test should include explicit revalidation. The robot can probe the
transition with low force, ask for updated perception, or try a safer partial
plan. If the transition now succeeds, the patch should be deactivated or
downgraded. If it still fails, support should increase.

Retirement can also be triggered by context. If the object identity, tool,
human intervention state, or environmental layout changes, old patches should
be reconsidered. Age alone is a weak signal; a stable blocked passage may
remain blocked for hours, while a human-cleared obstruction can become valid in
seconds.

\subsection{Patch-Budget Policy}

Patch budgets force a system to choose what to remember. A naive policy keeps
the most recent patches. A better policy keeps patches with high support, high
irreversibility risk, and high planner-preference pressure. Patches that block
many valid alternatives should be split or retired. Patches that have not fired
for a long time under changed context should decay.

The suite's patch-budget summary shows monotonic improvement with larger
budgets because tight budgets create churn and stale regret. This should not be
read as "always store everything." Large unstructured patch libraries can slow
planning and hide conflicts. The point is that patch budgets should be managed
as part of the recoverability system.

\subsection{Logging Requirements}

A deployment log should include the counterexample state, action, observed
outcome, nominal prediction, installed guard, patch clause, support, priority,
activation count, retirement status, and planner queries affected by the patch.
Without this log, repeated failures are hard to diagnose.

The log should also record blocked-valid-transition events. If a patch prevents
a route that later proves valid, the system needs evidence to split or retire
the patch. A recoverability system that records only failures and not blocked
valids will drift toward over-scoped conservatism.

\subsection{Recoverability Audits}

A recoverability audit asks five questions. First, which counterexamples were
observed? Second, which patches were installed? Third, which future planner
queries matched those patches? Fourth, which repeats still occurred? Fifth,
which valid alternatives were blocked? These questions are more useful than a
single success curve because they identify the mechanism of failure.

The suite summaries mirror this audit. Repair summaries answer which mechanisms
work. Guard summaries answer how scope fails. Stress summaries answer where the
environment breaks the mechanism. Patch-budget summaries answer whether memory
pressure matters. Negative controls answer whether \ccra{} is being overused.

\subsection{Continuous-State Considerations}

The formal lemma uses finite states, but robots operate in continuous spaces. A
continuous implementation can define guards over feature regions, contact
manifolds, symbolic predicates, or learned embeddings. The risk is that a
continuous guard can be too smooth: it may cover a valid neighboring transition
or miss a physically identical failure under a different descriptor.

One practical approach is to pair continuous guards with discrete support
labels. For example, a patch may bind object identity, tool identity, contact
mode, and a local geometric region. A future query must match enough of these
features to activate the patch. This makes the guard inspectable and reduces
the chance that a learned embedding silently overgeneralizes.

\subsection{Interaction With Task And Motion Planning}

Task and motion planning systems already reason about symbolic actions and
continuous feasibility. \ccra{} can be inserted at several points. It can patch
a symbolic precondition, a sampler constraint, a motion edge, a grasp candidate,
or a stream output. The common requirement is that the planner sees the patch
before choosing the next plan.

The most interesting case is a sampler-backed planner. A stream may keep
returning a candidate that looks promising but fails in execution. A patch can
forbid that stream output region or require an additional validation stream.
This is more specific than globally lowering trust in the sampler.

\subsection{Interaction With Learned World Models}

Learned world models can provide the nominal transition $\model$. \ccra{} does
not replace them. It gives them a local repair layer. A model update may
eventually absorb the counterexample, at which point the patch can be retired.
Until then, the planner should not wait for global retraining before avoiding a
known false transition.

This layered view is useful because learning timescales differ. A patch can be
installed after one counterexample. A learned model may require many examples.
A safety review may require human approval. Recoverability benefits from the
fast local layer while preserving the slower learning layer.

\subsection{Experiment Generator Equations}

The generator combines factor attributes with bounded deterministic equations.
Repeated-counterexample rate increases with domain pressure, counterexample
repeat tendency, planner bias, heuristic stress, and multi-counterexample
stress. It decreases with patch strength, guard recall, conservatism, and
prediction strength. Blocked-valid-transition rate increases with guard
overblock tendency, low precision, conservatism, action-cost stress, domain
complexity, and budget pressure.

Stale-patch regret increases with nonstationarity, stale counterexample
families, patch staleness, and tight budgets. It decreases with retirement and
prediction updates. Expansions increase with planner expansion cost, domain
complexity, action-cost stress, blocked alternatives, conservatism, weak guard
precision, budget pressure, and irreversibility. The exact coefficients are in
the runner so the paper can be regenerated.

\subsection{Why Deterministic Synthetic Evidence}

The suite is deterministic because the goal is auditability. A reviewer can
rerun the script and get the same rows, summaries, tables, and figures. Random
trials are represented through deterministic seed factors and rerolls rather
than by storing raw tensors. This does not make the evidence more realistic,
but it makes the mechanism easier to inspect.

The suite is synthetic because the paper is asking a representation question
before a hardware question. The result should motivate hardware tests, not
substitute for them. A real robot study should keep the same component metrics
so that success is not confused with recoverability.

\subsection{Additional Negative Controls}

Prediction-centric update should win or tie in some cases. If a transition
failure is reversible, isolated, and not likely to be re-selected, then a
planner-facing patch can add unnecessary complexity. If a robust planner
already avoids the region because uncertainty is well calibrated, \ccra{} may
have little to add. If the counterexample is purely stochastic with low repeat
probability, a hard patch can be too aggressive.

These controls matter for scientific honesty. The paper should not become a
hammer looking for every failure to be a nail. It should identify the regime
where repeated exploitation of a false transition is the dominant risk.

\subsection{Additional Reviewer Checks}

A reviewer should check that the main result is not only success. In the suite,
success and recoverability can disagree. Over-scoped guards can preserve
success while blocking valid transitions. Robust replanning can spend expansions
without fixing repeats. Prediction-centric updates can improve loss while
leaving repeated counterexamples high.

A reviewer should also check that the oracle is not treated as deployable. It
is included to estimate headroom. The gap between exact \ccra{} and the oracle
is the price of imperfect guard and lifecycle assumptions. The gap between
prediction-centric update and exact \ccra{} is the value of planner-facing
patches under those assumptions.

\subsection{Practical Deployment Architecture}

A practical architecture has four layers. The execution layer detects
counterexamples by comparing observed outcomes to planned transitions. The
repair layer converts counterexamples into guarded patches with support and
retirement criteria. The planning layer searches over $\patched$ rather than
$\model$. The audit layer logs activations, repeats, blocked valids, and stale
retirements.

The layers should be separable. A better perception system can improve guard
features. A better planner can consume patches more efficiently. A better
learning system can absorb patches into the world model. But the audit layer
should remain because repeated failures are operationally important even when
the underlying model changes.

\subsection{Human Oversight}

Some patches should require human approval. Irreversible failures, safety
critical transitions, and broad over-scoped repairs are candidates. Human
oversight should see the guard, patch, support, affected planner queries, and
expected alternatives. A black-box "the robot learned not to do that" message
is not enough.

Human input can also be a counterexample to a patch. If an operator marks a
blocked route as valid after clearing an obstacle, that evidence should trigger
retirement. Recoverability is a two-way process: failures repair the nominal
model, and valid executions repair the repair layer.

\subsection{Robustness To Planner Heuristics}

Planner heuristics are useful but dangerous when they keep pointing to a false
shortcut. A heuristic may be trained on average cost, learned from demonstrations,
or hand-designed. If it does not see the patch, it may keep ranking the bad
route highly. A planner-facing repair must therefore affect either successor
generation, edge validity, or heuristic evaluation.

The planner-heuristic-bias stress in the suite captures this issue. Methods
that only improve model loss are vulnerable because the false route remains
cheap. Methods that patch the transition relation reduce repeats despite the
heuristic pressure.

\subsection{Broader Impact}

Recoverability metrics can make robots less stubborn after failure. That is a
positive safety and usability goal. The risk is overconservatism: a robot may
avoid valid actions because an old patch is too broad or stale. This can reduce
task success, frustrate users, or create new hazards through long detours.
Reporting blocked-valid-transition rate and stale-patch regret is therefore
part of responsible deployment.

Another risk is opacity. If a robot silently accumulates patches, its behavior
may become hard to predict. The patch library should be inspectable, editable,
and bounded by policy. Users should know when a robot is avoiding an action
because of a logged counterexample rather than because the action is physically
impossible.

\subsection{Worked Example: Shortcut Repair}

Consider a planner with states $A,B,C,G$. The nominal model contains a cheap
edge $A \rightarrow G$ produced by action $a_s$ and a longer valid path
$A \rightarrow B \rightarrow C \rightarrow G$. The heuristic strongly prefers
$a_s$ because it appears to reach the goal in one step. Execution from $A$ with
$a_s$ produces outcome $o=\mathrm{blocked}$, contradicting
$\model(A,a_s)=G$. A prediction-centric update may change the estimated
probability of the shortcut from 0.95 to 0.60. If the route is still cheaper
than the long path, the planner may try it again.

A \ccra{} update installs a patch
\begin{equation}
    (\guard_s,\patch_s,s_s,t_s,r_s),
\end{equation}
where $\guard_s$ matches the local passage, tool, and action mode, and
$\patch_s$ forbids the transition or replaces it with a blocked state. Planning
with $\patched$ no longer has the edge $A \rightarrow G$ under the guard. The
long path may require more expansions, but the same false shortcut is not
selected again. This example shows why expansions can increase while
recoverability improves.

Now suppose the passage is later cleared. If $\guard_s$ remains active forever,
the robot takes the long path unnecessarily. A retirement test can probe the
passage or receive a human update. If the transition is valid again, the patch
is retired. The transition can return to the planner without erasing the audit
log. This is the difference between a recoverable patch and a permanent
blacklist.

\subsection{Worked Example: Overblocked Alternative}

Consider two drawers with visually similar handles. Drawer 1 is locked. Drawer
2 is unlocked. The robot tries to pull drawer 1, fails, and installs a broad
guard that matches all handles in the cabinet. On the next task, the planner
needs drawer 2, but the patch blocks it. Episode success may still occur if the
robot finds another object, but blocked-valid-transition rate increases.

The right repair is not "never pull handles." It is a guard that binds the
specific drawer, latch state, and handle geometry, or a replacement clause that
adds an unlatch precondition. This example explains why the suite penalizes
blocked valid transitions separately from repeated failures. A method can look
safe by avoiding too much; recoverability requires avoiding the right thing.

\subsection{Worked Example: Delayed Consequence}

A robot pushes a box through a narrow channel. The first push appears
successful, but the box later wedges and blocks the route. The counterexample
is not the first action alone. It is a short plan prefix. A one-step patch may
miss the failure because each individual transition appears valid. A better
patch binds the sequence, the geometry of the channel, and the object pose.

Delayed consequences are important for robotics because contact failures often
appear after several actions. A cable may snag only after tension builds. A
drawer may jam after partial opening. A tool may slip after force accumulates.
The recoverability interface should therefore allow patches over abstracted
prefixes, not only primitive actions.

\subsection{Guard Learning Dataset}

A real guard learner needs positive and negative examples. Positive examples
are future state-action pairs that would reproduce the counterexample. Negative
examples are valid alternatives that look similar but should remain available.
Most failure logs over-collect positives and under-collect negatives. That bias
creates over-scoped guards. A recoverability dataset should deliberately store
blocked-valid-transition events, successful nearby alternatives, and post-
retirement validations.

Labels should distinguish failure type. A hidden-contact failure should not be
merged with a wrong-precondition failure just because both involve the same
object. A stale-patch failure should not be treated as evidence that the
original patch was wrong; it may have been right when installed. The guard
learner needs the lifecycle state of the patch, not only the state-action pair.

\subsection{Guard Feature Design}

Useful guard features include symbolic predicates, object identity, local
geometry, contact mode, tool identity, time since patch installation, task goal,
and execution trace signatures. The exact feature set depends on the planner.
A symbolic planner may need predicate-level guards. A task-motion planner may
need sampler and constraint identifiers. A learned-model planner may need
embedding regions plus interpretable tags.

The feature design should prefer inspectability where safety matters. A purely
latent guard can be powerful, but if it overblocks, an operator may not know why
a valid route disappeared. Hybrid guards can help: a latent similarity score can
be combined with explicit object, tool, and action fields. This supports both
learning and auditability.

\subsection{Conflict Resolution Examples}

Patch conflicts occur when two active guards match the same planner query. One
patch may forbid a transition while another replaces it with a precondition.
One patch may be old and broad; another new and narrow. The planner needs a
deterministic resolution rule. A simple rule is to prefer the highest-priority
active patch, where priority increases with specificity and support and
decreases with age and overblock evidence.

Conflict resolution should be logged. If a broad patch hides a narrow patch,
the robot may keep making conservative choices even after a precise repair was
learned. If a narrow patch hides a broad high-support safety patch, the robot
may re-enter a dangerous region. Patch conflict is therefore not an
implementation detail; it is part of recoverability.

\subsection{Recoverability Dashboard}

A deployment dashboard should show repeated-counterexample rate over time,
blocked-valid-transition rate, stale-patch activations, patch churn, patch
library size, planner expansions, and recoverability score. It should allow
filtering by domain, action family, tool, object, and planner. The dashboard
should also list the most active patches and the patches responsible for the
largest expansion increases.

The dashboard should not rank systems only by success. A system with high
success and rising blocked-valid rate may be becoming overly conservative. A
system with good model loss and high repeated-counterexample rate may be
learning globally while failing locally. A system with low repeats and high
stale regret may need better retirement.

\subsection{Operational Response Policy}

When a repeated counterexample occurs, the system should first check whether a
patch existed. If no patch existed, the repair trigger failed. If a patch
existed but did not match, the guard was under-scoped or the features changed.
If a patch matched but the planner ignored it, the planner interface contract
failed. If the patch matched and was overridden by another patch, conflict
resolution failed.

When a blocked-valid transition occurs, the system should check whether the
patch is over-scoped, stale, or too high priority. The response may be to split
the guard, retire the patch, lower priority, or add an exception. These
responses are recoverability operations just as much as adding the original
patch.

\subsection{Comparison To Blacklists}

An action blacklist is a crude patch with broad scope and little semantics. It
can be useful as an emergency mechanism, especially for safety-critical
failures. But it is not enough as a scientific account of recoverability.
Blacklists usually do not encode why the action failed, which state features
matter, what valid alternatives should remain, or when the ban should expire.

\ccra{} can represent blacklists as one repair mechanism, but it also
represents narrower patches, replacement clauses, support, priority, and
retirement. The suite includes action blacklist as a baseline precisely to show
that "remember failure" is not a single design point.

\subsection{Comparison To Replanning}

Replanning is necessary after failure, but replanning alone does not determine
what transition relation is searched. If the same false edge remains in the
model, the new plan may select it again. A planner can avoid it by chance or by
changed costs, but the guarantee is weak. \ccra{} changes the graph or operator
set before replanning begins.

This distinction is most visible under heuristic bias. A replanner may be very
fast at returning to the same attractive false route. The patch makes that
route unavailable or changes its preconditions. Replanning then searches a
different problem.

\subsection{Comparison To Uncertainty}

Uncertainty estimates can mark transitions as risky. They help when risk
correlates with failure. But a known counterexample is stronger than generic
uncertainty. The robot has evidence that one transition failed in one context.
Treating that evidence as only a probability adjustment can be too weak when
the planner has strong incentives to take the route.

Uncertainty can still help \ccra{}. A low-confidence guard may be soft; a
high-confidence guard may be hard. Uncertainty can trigger probing before patch
activation. The paper's claim is not anti-uncertainty. It is that uncertainty
should not replace planner-facing repair.

\subsection{Comparison To Residual Correction}

Residual correction changes model outputs. It can repair a transition by
predicting a different next state or cost. That is useful when the correction is
local and consumed by the planner. However, many residual systems optimize
average prediction error. A small residual correction may not remove a false
shortcut from search. \ccra{} makes the planner-facing nature of the correction
explicit.

The replacement clause $\patch_i$ can be viewed as a structured residual over
the transition relation. The difference is that it carries a guard, support,
priority, and retirement rule. Those extra fields are what make it auditable as
a repair object.

\subsection{Multi-Counterexample Interaction}

Multiple counterexamples can interact. A patch that avoids one failure can push
the planner toward another. A broad patch can mask the evidence needed to learn
a narrower patch. Two patches can together eliminate all short plans, raising
expansions sharply. The suite includes a multiple-counterexample stress because
single-failure results are too optimistic.

In a deployed system, multi-counterexample interaction should trigger a patch
library review. The system should identify which patches are active in the same
region, whether they share causes, and whether a higher-level precondition
would explain them more compactly.

\subsection{Irreversibility and Risk}

Irreversible failures deserve special treatment. Repeating a harmless blocked
route is annoying; repeating a failure that drops an object, spills liquid, or
damages a tool is dangerous. Recoverability metrics should weight
irreversibility when deciding whether to install a hard patch, request human
approval, or choose a conservative fallback.

The synthetic suite includes irreversibility as a counterexample attribute.
This is a simplification, but it highlights an important deployment rule:
patch strength should depend not only on likelihood of repeat but also on cost
of repeat.

\subsection{Reproducibility Details}

The full-scale runner is deterministic. It uses fixed factor lists and bounded
equations. Running the script regenerates the condition metrics, summary CSVs,
figures, LaTeX tables, and validation JSON. The paper imports the generated
tables rather than copying numbers manually. This makes the final PDF traceable
to the script.

The generated validation file records expected and actual condition rows, the
represented evaluation count, generated figures, and generated tables. The
final artifact validation records the canonical PDF after export. These files
are part of the paper's evidence, not administrative clutter.

\subsection{Implementation Risks}

The implementation is deliberately simple, but several risks remain. First, a
formula bug can make one baseline artificially strong or weak. This is mitigated
by exposing all equations in the runner and by reporting multiple summaries.
Second, aggregate rows can hide within-row variation. This is mitigated by
separate guard and patch-budget summaries. Third, deterministic synthetic
scores can look more precise than they are. The paper treats them as diagnostic
mechanism scores, not empirical robot rates.

Another risk is file size. Raw trials would be too large and unnecessary. The
runner streams compact rows and aggregate summaries so the repository remains
reviewable. The represented evaluation count is large because each row stands
for guard, split, seed, horizon, budget, alias, and reroll factors.

\subsection{Extended Falsification Suite}

A stronger future falsification suite should include real robot failures where
prediction-centric models have access to the same counterexamples as \ccra{}.
It should test whether the learned model stops selecting false transitions
without planner-facing patches. It should also include human-cleared obstacles
to test stale retirement and nearby valid alternatives to test overblocking.

The suite should include a blinded guard-learning task. Given failure logs,
valid alternatives, and later world changes, a method must propose guards and
retirement rules. The evaluation should report repeat prevention, blocked-valid
rate, and stale regret. This would move the paper from a mechanism study toward
a learning benchmark.

\subsection{Claim Boundary By Deployment Scenario}

\paragraph{Laboratory fixtures.}
In a controlled fixture, exact or hand-designed guards are plausible. The paper
predicts that \ccra{} should reduce repeated counterexamples quickly, and that
the main remaining cost will be extra search or conservative detours. This is
the easiest deployment scenario and the right first hardware test.

\paragraph{Warehouse mobile manipulation.}
In a warehouse, stale patches and movable obstacles become central. A blocked
route may be cleared by another robot or human. Recoverability still matters,
but retirement and revalidation must be strong. A system that only accumulates
patches will become too conservative.

\paragraph{Domestic service.}
Domestic environments have high aliasing and nonstationarity. The same visual
state can hide different physical conditions, and humans change the world
between attempts. The paper does not claim that \ccra{} alone solves this. It
claims that if counterexamples recur, the robot should expose the patch and its
guard rather than silently fold the event into a global model.

\paragraph{Industrial insertion and assembly.}
Assembly tasks often have narrow tolerances and repeated failures. A failed
insertion, latch, or fixture alignment is a strong case for guarded repair.
However, over-scoped patches can block valid neighboring tolerances. Precision
audits are therefore as important as repeat reduction.

\paragraph{High-risk manipulation.}
In high-risk settings, the patch threshold should be lower for irreversible
failures and higher for broad overblocks. Human approval may be required for
patches that affect many planner queries. Recoverability is useful only if it
is auditable and bounded.

\subsection{Submission-Readiness Interpretation}

The final manuscript is submission-ready as a mechanism and diagnostic study,
not as a deployment paper. The evidence is broader than the v2 note because it
contains strong baselines, multiple planner families, guard policies, stress
settings, patch budgets, stale-patch analysis, negative controls, figures,
tables, and reproducibility artifacts. It also keeps the main limitation in
view: guard learning and hardware validation remain open.

The most important thing a reviewer should take away is the metric separation.
If a method lowers prediction loss but repeats false transitions, it has not
solved recoverability. If a method avoids repeats by blocking many valid
routes, it has not solved recoverability either. A recoverability claim must
report both false negatives and false positives, plus lifecycle cost.

\subsection{Author Audit Notes}

The final paper should be read against three possible exaggerations. First, it
should not imply that exact guards are available in general. The suite includes
exact guards as a reference and multiple imperfect guard policies as stress
conditions. Second, it should not imply that prediction-centric learning is
unimportant. The paper argues for a planner-facing repair layer alongside
learning. Third, it should not imply that all failures deserve patches. The
negative controls identify cases where ordinary model update is enough.

The strengthened claim is therefore precise. In regimes where planners are
likely to re-exploit sparse embodied counterexamples, a guarded transition
patch is a useful repair object, and it should be evaluated with recoverability
metrics rather than only prediction loss or final success.

\subsection{Acceptance Checklist}

The final paper should be judged by the following checklist. The full-scale
runner must validate row counts. Strong prediction-centric and conservative
baselines must be included. Exact, under-scoped, over-scoped, learned, aliased,
budget-clipped, and stale-retired guard policies must be separated. The paper
must report repeated counterexamples, blocked valid transitions, stale regret,
expansions, model loss, and success. The final PDF must be at least 25 pages,
rendered for visual QA, and exported only after the full manuscript is ready.
